Master the art of asking AI the right questions

Self-Paced ~45 minutes

Prerequisites: You should have completed Session 1 before starting this session.

Why This Matters at ATO

ATO tells every client that their team member arrives AI-enabled. Prompting is the foundation of that promise. When you know how to write a great prompt, you deliver faster, smarter results. That's what sets ATO apart.

Learn the fundamentals now, and you'll be ready for every client scenario on Day 1.

What This Session Covers

The difference between mediocre and great AI results comes down to one thing: how you write your prompts.

In this session, you'll learn the anatomy of a good prompt, see real-world examples, and practice writing prompts that get you exactly what you need—the first time.

The Anatomy of a Good Prompt

Every great prompt has five key parts. Here's the framework:

1. Role

Tell AI what role or perspective to take.

"You are an experienced executive assistant..."

Why? It sets context and shapes the tone of the response.

2. Context

Give AI background information about the situation.

"My client runs a small e-commerce business selling handmade jewelry. They're growing but under-resourced."

Why? Context makes the response specific and relevant to your situation.

3. Task

Be crystal clear about what you want.

"Write a follow-up email to a prospect who showed interest in our wholesale program."

Why? Clarity prevents wasted iterations.

4. Format

Specify how you want the output structured.

"Use bullet points. Keep it under 150 words. Start with a friendly greeting."

Why? Format requirements save you editing time and ensure usability.

5. Constraints

Tell AI what to avoid or exclude.

"Don't use the word 'synergy.' Avoid jargon. Don't mention pricing."

Why? Constraints keep the output on brand and prevent common missteps.

Before & After: Real Work Examples

Here's how the five-part framework transforms weak prompts into strong ones.

Example 1: Email Drafting (Client Scenario)

Weak Prompt

"Write an email about our services."

Problem: Too vague. AI doesn't know who the recipient is, what services matter, or what tone to use.

Strong Prompt

"You are an executive assistant for a tech startup founder. Write a professional but warm email to a potential investor who attended our pitch event last week. Mention the three key points we discussed: scalability, market fit, and our experienced team. Keep it to 3-4 sentences. Use a friendly, confident tone. Don't ask for money directly—just ask for a 15-minute call next week."

What Changed

  • Added role context (startup founder's assistant)
  • Specified the recipient and situation (investor, post-pitch)
  • Named specific content to include (three key points)
  • Set format and length (3-4 sentences)
  • Added tone direction (friendly, confident)
  • Included a constraint (don't ask for money directly)

Example 2: Research

Weak Prompt

"Tell me about social media trends."

Problem: Unclear scope. AI could write about any platform, any industry, any time period.

Strong Prompt

"I'm a marketing manager at a B2B SaaS company (project management software). Research the top 3 social media trends for 2024-2025 that are most relevant to B2B marketing. For each trend, explain why it matters and give one specific tactic we could test. Keep it to 200 words total. Avoid hype—focus on what's actually working. Format as a bullet-point list."

What Changed

  • Set the industry and role (B2B SaaS marketing manager)
  • Narrowed the scope (trends, not just generic info; 2024-2025 only)
  • Specified the output (3 trends, each with tactic)
  • Set a word limit (200 words)
  • Added a constraint on quality ("avoid hype")
  • Requested a format (bullet-point list)

Example 3: Meeting Prep (Client Scenario)

Weak Prompt

"Prepare for a client meeting."

Problem: Completely unclear. No details about the client, meeting goals, or prep format.

Strong Prompt

"You're my executive assistant. I have a 30-minute meeting with the VP of Sales at [Client Name], a mid-market software company. They want to discuss Q2 goals and team structure. I've worked with them for 2 years. Create a one-page prep doc with: (1) Key talking points for me to lead with, (2) Likely questions they'll ask and suggested answers, (3) 2-3 questions I should ask them. Use short, scannable sections. Assume I'm walking in with 5 minutes to review. Don't include anything I already know."

What Changed

  • Set the relationship and context (2-year relationship)
  • Specified the meeting goal (Q2 goals and team structure)
  • Named the format (one-page doc, specific sections)
  • Set usability constraints (scannable, 5-minute review time)
  • Added a quality constraint ("don't include what I already know")
  • Made it role-specific (assistant prep, not participant notes)

How to Iterate

Your first prompt won't always be perfect. That's OK. Here are four techniques to improve the output:

1. Be Direct

Tell AI exactly what's wrong and what to fix.

Example: "That email is too formal. Make it warmer and more conversational. Cut it to 3 sentences."

2. Give an Example

Show AI what good looks like.

Example: "Here's the tone I want: 'Hey Sarah, quick heads up—we've got some updates that I think you'll like. Let me know if you're free for a 10-min call.' Make the email sound like that."

3. Ask for Variations

Get multiple versions and pick the best one.

Example: "Give me three versions of this email: one very formal, one very casual, one somewhere in between."

4. Build on What Works

If the output is 80% right, keep it and refine the 20%.

Example: "Keep everything the same, but change the first paragraph to focus on ROI instead of features."

Giving Feedback Mid-Conversation

You're not locked into one prompt. During a conversation with AI, you can:

  • Correct: "That's not quite right. Actually, we're in Series B, not Series A."
  • Redirect: "Let's focus on the sales angle instead of the technical benefits."
  • Build On: "That's good. Now make it more concise and add a call-to-action at the end."
  • Challenge: "Is that assumption really true? Our customer data shows the opposite."

The best AI results come from conversation, not one-off prompts.

Practice Exercise (Required)

Time: 20–25 minutes

Your Task

Pick 3 tasks from the list below. For each task:

  1. Write a prompt using the five-part framework (Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints)
  2. Run the prompt in Claude (claude.ai)
  3. Review the output. Is it what you wanted?
  4. Iterate at least once using one of the four techniques
  5. Save your final prompt and output in a document

Task Options

  1. Draft a follow-up email to a prospect after an initial call
  2. Summarize a meeting recap (you provide the notes)
  3. Create a quick planning outline for your week
  4. Draft a proposal for a new client project
  5. Brainstorm 5 blog post ideas for a tech company
  6. Write a job description for an executive assistant role

What We're Looking For

  • Prompts that use the five-part framework clearly
  • Evidence that you reviewed and iterated
  • Outputs that are ready to use (or close to it)

Key Takeaways

  1. Structure matters. The five-part framework (Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints) works for nearly every prompt.
  2. Specificity is power. The more specific you are, the better the output. When in doubt, add more detail.
  3. Iteration is normal. You won't get it perfect the first time, and that's fine. Use the four techniques to refine.
  4. Conversation beats one-shot prompts. Talk to AI. Correct it. Build on it. That's where the magic happens.
  5. Quality in = Quality out. Spend 2 minutes writing a great prompt. Save 20 minutes on editing.
