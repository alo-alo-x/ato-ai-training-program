Session 5: Putting It Together

Demonstrate your AI competency and start delivering impact

Capstone Output required + 15–20 min review

Prerequisites

You should have completed Sessions 1–4 and submitted your practice work from Session 4 before starting this session.

What This Session Covers

This is the final session. You're going to create one document that shows how you'd actually use AI in your role. This is the competency check—not a test, but proof that you can use AI responsibly and effectively.

Estimated time: 45 minutes to create the document + 15–20 minutes for review call.

Before You Start

Gather these things from Sessions 2–4:

  • Your favorite prompts (from Sessions 2 and 3)
  • The practice work you completed in Session 4 (at least 3 sections)
  • Notes on what surprised you or what didn't work
  • Real examples of work you've done or could do with AI

You're not reinventing anything—you're just pulling together what you've already learned.

Your Final Output

Create a document (1–2 pages) with three required sections:

Section 1: How I'd Use AI in My Role

Pick 3–5 specific tasks that you actually do (or will do) in your role. For each task, include:

  1. Task name: Be specific. (Not "email management" but "triaging and replying to client emails")
  2. Your prompt: Write the exact prompt you'd use (or adapt from Sessions 2–4)
  3. Why AI helps: One sentence explaining the benefit. (Faster? Clearer? More options?)

Full Example: Weekly Client Status Update

Task: Create a weekly status email for clients

Prompt:

"Draft a weekly status email for a client. This week we completed: design refresh, wrote 3 blog posts, attended industry conference. Next week: launching new product page, final brand guidelines, stakeholder meeting. Tone: professional but warm. Keep to 250 words."

Why AI helps: I can draft the email in 2 minutes instead of 20, then customize it for the specific client's context.

Section 2: Sample Deliverable

Show one real piece of work that you completed using AI. Include both the prompt you used and the final output. Note what you edited and why.

Good examples: An email draft, a research summary, a meeting agenda, an organized dataset, a weekly plan.

What to Include

  1. The prompt you gave to AI (full text)
  2. The AI output (full or key excerpt)
  3. What you edited and why (2–3 sentences)
  4. How long it took you (total including edits)

Section 3: What I Learned

Reflection in 5–10 sentences. Cover these points:

  • What surprised you (good or bad)?
  • What didn't work, and why?
  • How would you explain AI use to a client?
  • What do you want to learn more about?
  • Which tool did you prefer and why?

Format Requirements

Keep It Real

  • Length: 1–2 pages (not a novel)
  • Format: Google Doc, Word, PDF, Notion—anything works
  • Include prompts: We need to see the actual prompts you used
  • Be specific: Don't generalize. Show real examples.
  • Honesty: If something didn't work, say so. That's more valuable than perfection.

The Review

Next step after you submit: A 15–20 minute conversation with Alli or Jas (your training leads).

What Happens in the Review

  1. You walk through your document (5 min)
  2. We ask follow-up questions (5–10 min)
  3. You get feedback and a pass/fail confirmation (2–5 min)

Example Review Questions

  • "Walk me through why you chose these 3 tasks. How often do you do them?"
  • "What would you do differently if the client said 'don't use AI'?"
  • "Show me where you edited the AI output. Why did you change it?"
  • "What was the biggest surprise working with AI?"
  • "How would you explain to a client that you used AI on this?"

What "Passing" Looks Like

Six Checkmarks You Need

  1. Understanding: You can explain how AI fits into your actual work (not a generic lecture)
  2. Clarity: Your prompts are specific and well-structured
  3. Judgment: You know when to use AI and when not to
  4. Editing: You review AI output and make deliberate changes
  5. Transparency: You acknowledge how AI was used and what you changed
  6. Reflection: You're thinking critically about what works and what doesn't

What If You Don't Pass?

This is not punishment. This is a safety net.

What Happens

  • Specific feedback: We tell you exactly what to improve and why
  • Redo timeline: You have 48 hours to revise and resubmit
  • 1:1 coaching (if needed): If you're stuck, we schedule a 20-minute call to help

Real talk: Most people pass on the first try. The ones who don't usually just need to be more specific about their examples. You've got this.

Submission

How to Submit

  1. Create your document (Google Doc, Word, PDF, Notion)
  2. Share it with your ATO point person (Alli, Jas, or team lead)
  3. Include a note: "Ready for Session 5 review"
  4. They'll schedule your 15–20 minute review call within 2 business days

After You Pass

You're officially AI-competent at ATO. Here's what comes next:

Tool Upgrade

  • Claude Pro: Faster responses, longer context, earlier access to new features
  • Cowork: Ability to upload files, use web search, and build workflows
  • Access to both: Fully enabled in your ATO tools

Continuing Education

  • Monthly "AI Office Hours" with the training team
  • Access to advanced prompt library (as it grows)
  • First look at new ATO AI resources and playbooks

Keep Building

  • Start using AI on real client work (with transparency)
  • Document your workflows (what prompts work best?)
  • Share wins with the team (we learn together)

Real talk: This training is the start, not the finish. AI is changing fast. The people who win aren't the ones who passed training—they're the ones who keep experimenting, learning, and improving. Keep building.

Key Takeaways From This Program

  • AI is a tool for thinking, not a replacement for judgment. You stay in control.
  • The best prompts are specific. Vague questions get vague answers.
  • Always review and edit. AI is fast but not always right.
  • Transparency builds trust. Tell clients how you work.
  • Your workflow matters more than any single tool. Build systems that last.
